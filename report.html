Assessment

Over 90 days, the data tells a story of progressive fitness adaptation at the edge of sustainable recovery. Sleep scores average 74.5, HRV has climbed from 43 to 52 ms, and nightly recharge has improved each successive month — all pointing to a body that is genuinely getting fitter.

Yet the same data reveals a structural tension: sleep duration is shrinking by roughly 14 minutes per month, the Skar loop is being ridden 15 minutes faster than in December, and each of the three Vestby rides has been harder than the last — with increasingly poor recovery following each one.

The core risk is accumulated load without deload stimulus. There is not a single two-day rest gap across all 247 activities. The March 21–26 block — five Skar rides in six days at record intensity — produced the worst sleep night of the entire period. The body tolerates consecutive hard days, but eventually presents the bill.

The late-night training habit (10 Skar rides after 20:00, mobility sessions routinely past 21:00) compresses the recovery window and pushes average bedtime to 01:09. The clearest positive trend in the entire dataset is the gradual shift toward earlier nights — down from 02:36 in December to 00:53 in March. This single behavioural change explains most of the March recharge improvement.
